Warm Front

A warm front is when a moist, warm air mass goes over a cold air mass. Since there is a large amount of warm air, the warm air condenses with the cold air to create some rainfall. After moving through, the warm air replaces the cold air at the surface.

Cold Front

A cold front is when a cooler, drier air mass goes over a warmer air mass. Since cold air is heavier than warm air, the cool air stays near the surface. This can create large thunderstorms and is usually associated with stronger weather. A lot of times the cold air stays near the surface and the warmer air stays above.

Stationary Front

A stationary front is similar to a warm front but is when warm air is present behind the front (south) and cold air is in front of it (north). A stationary front is different as the cooler air does not move and storms continue to form near the boundary. This occurs sometimes during the summer with prolonged periods of thunderstorms.

Occluded Front

An occluded front occurs when a cold airmass catches up with a warm air mass and there is currently a cooler air mass in place moving through. Both the cool and cold airmasses push the warm air upwards or “occluded.” This front most likely occurs during cyclogenesis or the intensification of a cyclone (low pressure system)

Rain

Moisture that is condensed in the atmosphere that creates distinct visible drops.

Snow

Water vapor that is frozen into ice crystals that fall down and accumulate as a white layer.

Freezing Rain

Rain that freezes on contact with the surface. Occurs when there is only a shallow layer of cold air near the surface.

Sleet

Rain that does not have enough time to refreeze into snow, but can freeze into small ice pellets. Sleet can sound very loud when heavy amounts are falling.

Hail

It is formed through thunderstorm updrafts where layers of water freeze to form large balls of ice. Hail can range from 0.25” to 8”.

Graupel

It is formed when supercooled water is stuck to falling snow to create small soft pellets. The pellets are between 2-5mm.

Dew Point

The Dew point is an important factor in weather forecasting and daily forecasts. It can determine the moisture content in the atmosphere. The higher the dew point, the more moisture there is in the atmosphere. When the temperature and dew point are the same temperature, the water can condense and form precipitation.
